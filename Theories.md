# Theories.md — Core Advertising & Marketing Theories (2024–2026 Edition)

> Part of the [AdMarketing-Theories-2026](./README.md) repository.
> This document provides in-depth analysis of foundational and emergent theories in advertising and marketing, contextualised within the 2024–2026 era of LLMs, agentic AI, and platform fragmentation.

---

## 1. Dual Process Theory (System 1 / System 2)

**Theoretical Origin:** Kahneman (2011), building on Stanovich & West (2000)

**Core Proposition:** Human cognition operates via two parallel systems. System 1 is fast, automatic, heuristic-driven, and emotionally responsive. System 2 is slow, deliberate, effortful, and analytically rigorous. In advertising, this duality determines how persuasive messages are processed — whether via peripheral cues (System 1) or substantive argument evaluation (System 2).

**2026 Extension:** With the proliferation of AI-generated content and recommendation systems, the distinction has grown more consequential. LLM-generated ad copy is architected to activate System 1 responses through emotionally resonant language patterns, while simultaneously providing System 2-compatible factual scaffolding for high-involvement purchasers. This dual-targeting approach, often called **"Cognitive Bifurcation Optimization,"** is a distinguishing feature of AI-driven creative strategy in 2026.

Furthermore, the rise of **zero-click search results** (where AI answers displace traditional organic links) compresses the decision window so severely that System 2 engagement is often bypassed entirely, raising critical ethical questions about AI-mediated consent.

**Key Dimensions:**
- System 1 activation: visual salience, brand familiarity, social proof signals
- System 2 activation: comparative pricing, specification tables, review depth
- AI implication: Generative models fine-tuned on behavioral response data can now predict which system will dominate for a given user segment

## 2. Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM)

**Theoretical Origin:** Petty & Cacioppo (1986)

**Core Proposition:** Attitude change occurs through one of two persuasion routes. The **central route** involves high cognitive elaboration — careful evaluation of message arguments. The **peripheral route** relies on heuristic cues such as source attractiveness, social consensus, and credibility signals. The model predicts that attitudes formed via the central route are more durable and resistant to counter-persuasion.

**2026 Extension:** ELM undergoes significant reinterpretation in AI-mediated environments. **Algorithmic Persuasion** systems dynamically route users between peripheral and central processing paths based on real-time behavioral signals. A user exhibiting low scroll depth and rapid swipe patterns is identified as peripheral-route susceptible; content is immediately recalibrated to prioritize vivid imagery, influencer endorsements, and scarcity cues.

Critically, the emergence of **virtual influencers** as peripheral-route cue providers complicates ELM's source credibility assumptions. When users know a source is AI-generated, the peripheral cue's effectiveness depends on the **CASA Paradigm** — whether users extend social actor expectations to synthetic entities.

The 2026 implication: ELM must be extended with a third "**Algorithmic Route**" — processing delegated entirely to an AI agent that evaluates arguments on the user's behalf, with the agent's recommendation serving as a meta-peripheral cue.

## 3. Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB)

**Theoretical Origin:** Ajzen (1991)

**Core Proposition:** Behavioral intention — the proximate predictor of action — is determined by three antecedents: (1) **Attitude** toward the behavior (positive/negative evaluation), (2) **Subjective Norms** (perceived social pressure from relevant others), and (3) **Perceived Behavioral Control** (PBC — the sense of ease or difficulty in performing the behavior).

**2026 Extension:** TPB acquires new operational dimensions in AI-augmented consumer environments:

**Attitude:** AI-generated personalized content systematically shapes attitudinal valence before conscious deliberation occurs. Repeated exposure to emotionally optimized creatives primes attitude formation, complicating TPB's assumption of conscious attitude evaluation.

**Subjective Norms:** The influencer economy has industrialized norm transmission. In 2026, norms are conveyed not only by human social networks but by **AI-curated "social proof feeds"** — algorithmically assembled testimonials, user-generated content snippets, and virtual influencer endorsements designed to simulate normative consensus.

**Perceived Behavioral Control:** The introduction of **one-click and in-context AI purchasing agents** fundamentally reduces perceived difficulty. When an AI agent can complete a transaction conversationally, PBC barriers (payment friction, search effort, comparison complexity) are eliminated, dramatically accelerating conversion from intention to action.

TPB's predictive validity in 2026 is therefore highest when modeling **AI-agent-mediated behavior**, where all three antecedents can be simultaneously optimized by the same system.

## 4. Social Learning Theory (Observational Learning)

**Theoretical Origin:** Bandura (1977, 1986)

**Core Proposition:** Behavior is acquired through observation, imitation, and vicarious reinforcement. Four sub-processes govern this: **Attention**, **Retention**, **Motor Reproduction**, and **Motivation** (or reinforcement expectation). In marketing, social learning explains how consumers adopt new products by observing peer usage and aspirational models.

**2026 Extension:** Social Learning Theory undergoes a fundamental structural transformation in the creator economy era. The traditional **model-observer dyad** is now a **content algorithm-consumer triad** — the algorithm selects which behavioral models are shown to which observers, based on predicted imitation probability.

**Virtual influencers** represent a novel class of behavioral model: computationally optimized to demonstrate product usage in maximally imitable, aspirationally achievable ways. Unlike human models, their behavior is never accidental — every gesture, context, and lifestyle signal is engineered for observational impact.

The **De-influencing movement** (2023–2025) introduced a significant disruptive counter-current: creators deliberately modeling non-consumption behavior, demonstrating product rejection, and signaling authenticity through restraint. Social Learning Theory must now account for the imitation of **abstention behaviors** as a commercially relevant phenomenon, particularly in sustainability-oriented segments.

## 5. Schema Theory in Brand and VR Contexts

**Theoretical Origin:** Bartlett (1932), Rumelhart (1980), Mandler (1982)

**Core Proposition:** Schemas are cognitive knowledge structures that organize information about objects, events, people, and roles. They enable rapid interpretation of new stimuli by mapping them onto pre-existing categorical frameworks. In brand contexts, **brand schemas** encode consumer expectations about product attributes, personality dimensions, and usage contexts.

**2026 Extension — Brand-Schema Alignment in Virtual Reality:**

VR and immersive metaverse environments present a uniquely complex schema challenge: brands must achieve **cross-reality schema consistency** — ensuring that the cognitive structures consumers hold about a brand in physical retail translate coherently into virtual environments without generating **schema incongruity dissonance**.

When a brand's virtual environment (avatar aesthetics, spatial design, interaction mechanics) diverges significantly from its established schema, consumers experience cognitive friction, reducing brand equity. Conversely, brands that achieve **schema augmentation** — using VR's interactive affordances to add new, consistent brand dimensions — demonstrate significantly higher post-experience purchase intent.

In 2026, **spatial brand schemas** are an emerging construct: the consumer's cognitive map of how a brand is meant to feel in three-dimensional, embodied space. Luxury brands investing in metaverse flagship experiences are operationalizing spatial schema design as a core brand architecture discipline.

**LLM Implication:** Generative AI models encode brand schemas as probabilistic co-occurrence patterns in training data. When an LLM generates brand-associated content, it reproduces prevailing schemas or, if prompted adversarially, can generate schema-incongruent narratives that damage brand perception at scale.

## 6. Attribution Theory in the Age of De-influencing and AI Transparency

**Theoretical Origin:** Heider (1958), Kelley (1967, 1973)

**Core Proposition:** Attribution Theory examines how individuals assign causality to behaviors and outcomes. Kelley's **ANOVA model** posits that observers use consistency, distinctiveness, and consensus to make internal (dispositional) or external (situational) attributions. In advertising, consumers attribute commercial intent to persuasive communications, affecting persuasion outcomes through **persuasion knowledge activation** (Friestad & Wright, 1994).

**2026 Extension — De-influencing and AI Transparency:**

The **De-influencing phenomenon** (2023–present) constitutes a large-scale natural experiment in Attribution Theory dynamics. When content creators publicly identify and reject products they previously endorsed, consumer attributions shift dramatically. The creator is attributed with **high distinctiveness** (unusual behavior relative to influencer norms) and **high consistency** (repeated honest review pattern), producing strong internal/dispositional attribution — the creator is perceived as genuinely principled, not commercially motivated.

This attribution shift paradoxically **enhances source credibility** for de-influencers while simultaneously **activating persuasion knowledge** about the broader influencer economy.

**AI Transparency Dimension:** The EU AI Act (effective 2025–2026) mandates disclosure of AI-generated content in advertising. When consumers are informed they are being addressed by an AI system, attribution processes are disrupted: they cannot apply traditional consensus/consistency/distinctiveness heuristics to a non-human actor. Emergent research suggests consumers make **system-level attributions** — attributing commercial intent to the brand deploying the AI rather than the AI itself — a finding with significant implications for brand trust architecture.

**Practical Implication:** Brands must design AI transparency disclosures that manage attribution outcomes proactively, framing AI use as a tool for personalization (benefiting the consumer) rather than as persuasion automation (serving corporate interests).

## 7. Uses and Gratifications Theory — AI Agent User Motivations

**Theoretical Origin:** Katz, Blumler & Gurevitch (1974)

**Core Proposition:** Users are active, goal-directed media consumers who select media based on expected gratifications. The original gratification typology encompasses: **information/cognitive gratifications**, **personal identity reinforcement**, **social integration**, and **entertainment/diversion**. The theory emphasizes user agency and purposive media selection.

**2026 Extension — AI Agent Gratifications:**

Uses and Gratifications Theory undergoes its most significant reformulation when applied to **AI agent interactions**. Users engaging with AI assistants (ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Claude) exhibit a distinct gratification profile that diverges substantially from traditional media consumption:

**Cognitive Efficiency Gratification:** Users engage with AI agents primarily to collapse information-seeking effort — outsourcing research, comparison, and decision preparation. This "cognitive offloading gratification" was absent in traditional media theory.

**Autonomy Preservation Gratification:** Counter-intuitively, users report feeling *more* in control when delegating to AI agents — the agent mediates complexity without removing the final decision authority. This perceived agency paradox has significant implications for AI-mediated marketing touchpoint design.

**Relational Simulation Gratification:** Especially among younger demographics, AI agents satisfy needs for non-judgmental dialogue, responsive engagement, and personalized attention previously met only by human relationships or parasocial media bonds. Brands deploying conversational AI must design for this gratification dimension or risk interaction abandonment.

**Competence Signaling Gratification:** Using sophisticated AI tools has acquired social capital in professional and aspirational consumer communities. Brands that enable sophisticated AI-mediated discovery and purchase are inadvertently aligning with this identity-reinforcement gratification.

The 2026 reformulation proposes a **five-dimensional AI Gratification Model**: Efficiency, Autonomy, Relational, Competence, and **Serendipity** — the gratification derived from AI-surfaced discoveries that the user would not have sought independently.

## 8. Information Processing Theory in the Age of LLMs

**Theoretical Origin:** Miller (1956), Broadbent (1958), Craik & Lockhart (1972)

**Core Proposition:** The human mind processes information through sequential stages — **Sensory Register → Short-Term/Working Memory → Long-Term Memory** — with attention as the critical bottleneck. Processing depth (shallow/superficial vs. deep/semantic) determines memory trace strength and subsequent retrieval probability.

**2026 Extension — LLM-Mediated Information Architecture:**

The classical Information Processing model was constructed around human cognitive constraints. LLMs introduce a fundamentally different information processing architecture that operates as both a **mediator** of consumer cognition and a **competitor** for information processing primacy:

**Attention Compression:** LLMs — through zero-click answers, AI summaries, and generative search results — intercept information-seeking behaviors before stimuli reach the consumer's sensory register. Brands that fail to achieve inclusion in LLM-generated answers are cognitively pre-filtered, never reaching the consumer's working memory.

**Semantic Encoding Shift:** Traditional advertising aimed for deep semantic encoding through emotional resonance, narrative structure, and repetition. In 2026, brands must also optimize for **LLM semantic encoding** — ensuring that brand narratives, product attributes, and value propositions are encoded in LLM training data (and retrieval-augmented contexts) with sufficient weight to influence generated content.

**Retrieval Augmentation:** The emergence of **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)** architectures means that brand information lives in two parallel encoding systems simultaneously — human long-term memory and AI vector databases. Brands that invest in structured data, semantic markup, and authoritative content are investing in both human and machine cognitive infrastructure simultaneously.

**Collective Working Memory:** LLMs represent a form of exocortical working memory — a shared cognitive resource that increasingly handles the working memory functions (comparison, synthesis, evaluation) that classical theory assigned to individual human minds. Marketing strategy must now account for influencing this collective layer.
